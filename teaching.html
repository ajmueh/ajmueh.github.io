Teaching Philosophy

I believe that the most important concept a student can internalize is that of "self-efficacy", or the belief in one's ability to overcome challenges, learn new things, and create something from nothing. This concept is particularly important in a field such as Data Science, where methods are always evolving, can be complex, and are diverse and idiosyncratic to specific subfields. I consider it key then that students develop not just a comprehensive toolkit of core skills, but the capacity and willingness to learn new skills on their own. For this reason, there are three practices that structure my approach to teaching:

  1. Assignment design that leads instead of pushes

    2. When teaching data and scientific literacy, the primary goal should be to achieve higher-order learning objectives i.e. to transition from reiteration/memorization to synthesis/creation. Assignment design that initially demands little of students, but progressively encourages them to apply what they've learned in increasingly unstructured ways is a powerful way to achieve such outcomes.

  2. Instructor modeling of authentic practice

    3. While many students will learn to succeed in the classroom, they rarely have the opportunity to practice their skills in a more realistic setting. I try to model workflows authentic to science and industry in the classroom, demonstrate practical skills for overcoming regular challenges they can expect to encounter, and provide students a broader context for what they're learning.

  3. Facilitation of positive psychology

    4. Self-efficacy is an important component of success in Data Science, but who has an intrinsic sense of self-efficacy and who needs time to develop that sense is unevenly distributed. Many students from a variety of backgrounds must overcome anxieties and negative perceptions of themselves before they can flourish. To mitigate this disparity, I use active engagement in the classroom, proactive interaction with students, acknowledgement of the often challenging nature of the material, and consistent reminder that the material is tractable.
